First of all, New Zealand is an amazing place - incredibly beautiful, with a very friendly vibe. We had the privilege of running a bootcamp down in Christchurch, as part of the eResearch NZ 2013 conference.

The eResearch event brings together a fairly diverse set of researchers across New Zealand. The goal of the event is to provide them with a venue for "connecting and collaborating on their computational and data related research". Software Carpentry was invited to help teach a workshop for the duration of the event to help scientists build better software.

We broke from the traditional bootcamp format to help us fit into the event. We ran 4 half-day afternoon sessions:

  • Day 0 (optional): Introduction to the Shell.
  • Day 1: Shell Redux and Version Control with Git.
  • Day 2: Introduction to Programming in Python and Scientific Python.
  • Day 3: Software Testing and Reproducible Science
The more spaced out time-table was actually quite nice: it gave participants a bit more time to digest the information, and it allowed instructors to prepare and tweak the oncoming sessions, depending on how the previous sessions went (agile bootcamps!). The main drawback was that some people didn't attend all sessions, because there were other workshops going on in parallel. For participants who wandered between them, maintaining continuity was a challenge. Also, cramming all that Python into one afternoon (from "Hello World" to Numpy Matrices) might have felt like drinking out of the firehose.

Overall, the audience was pretty mixed. We had the usual set of novice post-docs and graduate students. But we had at least a few computationally-experienced participants who were there to learn what this kind of thing looks like. Nevertheless, most participants learned some new things from the sessions, and we definitely learned a few things just from being there.

The good news is that the parts that really seemed to resonate were the ideas behind reproducible science, good software design and maintaining version history. While we introduced people to a lot of tools - the take home message was about using these tools in the right way.

A big thanks to Nick Jones, Tim McNamara and the rest of the NeSI team for helping us put on the event.
